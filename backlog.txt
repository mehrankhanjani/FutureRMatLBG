Overall, the prototype should tell one connected story: Daisy identifies the opportunity before the client asks, activates intelligence for the meeting, uses live prompts to shape the conversation, structures a stronger proposition, mobilises the bank instantly, manages the deal through agents and strengthens the relationships needed to win.
 

here is a narrative:
The flow should start before the meeting, with an Opportunity Ignition moment. This shows why Daisy is engaging the client in the first place. The system surfaces a high-value prospect or existing client opportunity based on market movement, client behaviour, peer activity, portfolio performance or upcoming liquidity / funding events. This moment should answer: ‚ÄúWhy this client, why now, and why are we ahead of competitors?‚Äù Prospecting becomes signal-led rather than manually driven. Daisy is guided towards the clients and opportunities most likely to convert, with estimated value, urgency, rationale and suggested next action. 

 

The next moment is Consent / Activate Intelligence. This should not feel like an admin gate. It should feel like Daisy switching on live intelligence for the meeting. Consent unlocks real-time signal detection, opportunity shaping, risk identification and automated follow-up. If consent is declined, Daisy can still continue manually, but with reduced live intelligence. The message should be: consent enables the bank to convert the conversation into action, while keeping the RM in control. 

 

The third moment is the Live Client Moment. As Daisy speaks with the client, the platform detects intent, urgency, risk and opportunity in real time. This should move beyond transcription. The system should surface prompts that help Daisy shape the conversation while it is happening ‚Äî for example, expansion into Germany, liquidity pressure, FX exposure, working capital need or timing risk. The prompt should show commercial value, why the signal matters, what Daisy could say next and how acting now creates advantage before competitors engage. 

 

The fourth moment should be a separate Deal Structuring screen. This is important because it brings the ‚Äúwinning clients‚Äù angle to life. Once the opportunity is detected, Daisy should not just receive a follow-up task ‚Äî the platform should help shape the best proposition. This screen could show recommended deal structures, options to compare, pricing / risk considerations, product combinations, likely client fit, internal constraints and competitor positioning. For CIB, this could include financing strategy, hedging options, capital structure or RFP positioning. For BCB, this could include working capital, lending, deposits, FX, payments or growth funding. The key message is: Daisy is supported to shape a better answer before the client formally asks or goes to market. 

The fifth moment is Post-Meeting Momentum. The meeting should immediately convert into structured outputs: key decisions, client intent, commercial opportunity, follow-up communication, internal actions and CRM / pipeline updates. The screen should feel less like ‚Äúsummary written automatically‚Äù and more like ‚Äúopportunity now progressing‚Äù. It should show what happens next, who is mobilised, what is needed, what is due today and what risk exists if action is delayed. The value is not just fewer notes ‚Äî it is faster conversion from client conversation into executable sales motion. 
 

The sixth moment is Agent Bench / Deal Orchestration. This should show Daisy orchestrating the deal rather than doing the work manually. A bench of agents should progress activity in the background: credit readiness, product input, data gathering, pricing support, meeting prep, internal coordination, fulfilment initiation and client follow-up. Daisy should only be pulled in where judgement, challenge or relationship ownership is required. This screen should make the distinction clear: agents handle execution; Daisy handles judgement, trust and deal direction. This aligns strongly to the proposal‚Äôs ambition for CRM and AI to reduce manual work, improve prioritisation and increase RM client-facing time 
.  

The seventh moment is Relationship Intelligence / Win Strategy. This should not be a static relationship graph. It should show where Daisy is strong, where she is exposed and what relationships need to be built to win the opportunity. For example, if the German expansion opportunity requires Treasury engagement, the graph should highlight missing coverage and recommend introductions. This is especially important for CIB, where access, influence and multi-stakeholder engagement can make the difference between being in the process and shaping the process. The message should be: deals are won through relationship coverage as much as product capability. 
  

The final moment can return to Portfolio Opportunity Radar, showing that this is not a one-off journey. Daisy‚Äôs whole book is continuously monitored for signals, risks, whitespace and next-best-action. The system prioritises where she should spend time today based on value, urgency and likelihood to progress. This reinforces the shift from reactive portfolio coverage to always-on origination. It links directly to the future sales journey ambition in the proposal: AI-ranked client opportunities, tailored client preparation, origination, internal mobilisation, lending progression and automated outcome capture.

# Scene Talk-Track — How to Present Every Step

> A shared narration for the 10-scene prototype (the Avonmore / M&A story).
> The goal: **anyone on the team can stand up and explain any step** — what it is,
> what it does, and the point it lands — without having built it.
>
> Each scene has four parts:
> - **In one sentence** — the single line to lead with
> - **What's on screen** — what the audience is looking at
> - **The talk-over** — the words to say, roughly as spoken
> - **The point** — the one idea to make sure lands before you move on
>
> Keep it conversational. The prototype is the proof; your job is the story around it.

---

## The story in 30 seconds (say this once, up front)

**Daisy Bennett** is a Senior Relationship Manager. For seven years she's covered
**Avonmore Components**, a Midlands manufacturer — her main contact is the Group CFO,
**Sarah Whitfield**. This week Avonmore has quietly **acquired Calderwood Engineering**,
a precision-engineering firm that Lloyds *also* banks — but through a different RM,
**Marcus Reed**, in a different coverage team.

So two Lloyds clients have just become **one group — the Avonmore Group** — a roughly
**£12m acquisition-and-integration mandate**. That is a moment of both opportunity and
risk, and today it depends entirely on whether Daisy spots it and moves before competitors do.

Running underneath all of this is **REO** — the orchestrating intelligence. REO is not
a chatbot and not "a bunch of agents". It's the always-on layer that watches, surfaces,
drafts and coordinates — while **Daisy stays in charge of the relationship and the judgement**.

The 10 scenes follow that one deal from first signal to closed mandate and into the next cycle.

**The cast, at a glance:**

| Who | Role |
|---|---|
| Daisy Bennett | Senior RM, our protagonist — covers Avonmore |
| Sarah Whitfield | Group CFO of Avonmore — the client in the room |
| Avonmore Components | Daisy's 7-year client, the acquirer |
| Calderwood Engineering | The acquired firm — already a Lloyds client |
| Marcus Reed | The other RM, covers Calderwood (different team) |
| REO | The orchestrating AI — the enabler behind every scene |

---

### Scene 1 · Opportunity Ignition
*A reason to call today — surfaced and qualified before you dial.*

**In one sentence.** REO gives Daisy a reason to pick up the phone today — and the
evidence for why now is the moment.

**What's on screen.** An "ignition" alert at the top of Daisy's screen: the headline
event (Avonmore has acquired Calderwood), a confidence level, an estimated opportunity
value, and a grid of "why this, why now" signals — each tagged with where it came from
(Companies House filing, internal MI, market data).

**The talk-over.**
> "This is where the day starts. Daisy already knows Avonmore inside out — seven years
> of relationship. What she *doesn't* know yet is that overnight the picture changed:
> her client has just acquired another company Lloyds already banks. REO caught the
> change-of-control filing before it hit the news, connected it to the relationship Daisy
> already owns, and surfaced it as a single alert — *this* client, *now*, and here's *why*.
> Notice it's not a raw data dump: it leads with the relationship context, then shows the
> signal, the estimated value, and the confidence — every claim traceable back to a cited
> source. Daisy hasn't done any research. She's just been handed a qualified reason to act."

**The point.** We're not replacing the RM's relationship — we're making sure she never
misses the moment. Proactive, evidence-based, and she moves first.

---

### Scene 2 · Relationship Intelligence
*The full group picture — before you walk in.*

**In one sentence.** Before the meeting, Daisy sees the whole relationship — seven years
of it — in one place.

**What's on screen.** A relationship view for the Avonmore Group: a health score, a
12-month sentiment trend, a network map of contacts across both Avonmore and Calderwood,
and a full interaction history across channels (meetings, calls, emails, CRM notes) —
with a marker for what's shared with the team versus Daisy's personal notes.

**The talk-over.**
> "The ignition alert told Daisy *why now*. This scene gives her the *context* before she
> walks in. It's not a point-in-time snapshot — it's the whole relationship: how healthy
> it is, how sentiment has moved over the last year, who the key contacts are across both
> companies, and every touchpoint that's ever happened. Crucially, this includes Marcus's
> side — the Calderwood contacts — so for the first time she's looking at *one group*, not
> two separate files. And this knowledge belongs to the bank, not to Daisy's memory or her
> inbox. If she were off tomorrow, whoever picked this up would have all of it."

**The point.** The relationship is the asset, and it's owned by the bank — not trapped in
one person's head. This is the "human relationship first, technology enables" framing.

---

### Scene 3 · Activate Intelligence
*Walking into the room — consent on, live support ready.*

**In one sentence.** With the client's consent, Daisy switches on live support for the
conversation — and if they decline, notes mode does the same job by hand.

**What's on screen.** A mobile-style consent gate: a clear confirmation that the client
has agreed to the meeting being captured, with the flagged M&A event shown as a pre-read.
If consent isn't given, a notes-capture fallback (typed, voice-to-text or handwriting).

**The talk-over.**
> "Now Daisy's in the room. Before anything is captured, consent is confirmed — visibly,
> honestly. The framing matters: this isn't surveillance, it's *enabling a better
> conversation*. With consent on, REO can listen and give Daisy live support. And if the
> client says no? That's fine — notes mode captures the same information manually, and REO
> structures it into exactly the same outputs afterwards. Daisy stays in control the whole
> time, and the client always knows what's happening."

**The point.** Trust first. The capability works *with* consent, degrades gracefully
without it, and the human is always in control.

---

### Scene 4 · Live Client Moment
*Sarah names the need — REO shapes it in the instant.*

**In one sentence.** The moment the client voices a need, REO recognises it, shapes it
into an opportunity, and tells Daisy what to say next.

**What's on screen.** A live transcript with speaker attribution and a recording timer.
As Sarah speaks, REO classifies the conversation live — intent, risk, opportunity,
context, emotion — and surfaces an in-the-moment prompt: the next best question and the
commercial rationale behind it. A draggable panel lets Daisy hide the AI to stay present.

**The talk-over.**
> "This is the heart of it. Sarah, the CFO, starts talking about refinancing the acquisition
> and bringing the two businesses' banking together. The old way, Daisy makes a mental note
> and reconstructs it later. Here, the instant Sarah names the need, REO recognises three
> things *at once* — the intent to refinance and consolidate, the risk of fragmented
> ownership and banking, and the shape of the deal: acquisition finance, cash, FX. And it
> doesn't just record it — it hands Daisy the *next question* and the reason to ask it. Need
> and opportunity surface as a single moment, not two separate steps. And she can swipe the
> AI away any time to stay eye-to-eye with her client."

**The point.** Revenue gets shaped *in the room*, not rebuilt from notes afterwards — and
the AI makes the human a better listener, not a distracted one.

---

### Scene 5 · Deal Structuring & Win Strategy
*Shape the right facility before it goes to credit.*

**In one sentence.** REO proposes three deal structures built around the client's
situation, and Daisy picks the one that serves Avonmore best.

**What's on screen.** Three structure options, each with facility components, indicative
terms, indicative economics, and — importantly — an indicative credit-eligibility status
(approvable / needs review / needs escalation). An "explore" vs "committed" toggle, a
"why this structure" rationale, and a one-click "loop in a product specialist" action.

**The talk-over.**
> "Now Daisy shapes the deal. REO gives her three options — but notice how they're framed:
> each one is built around *Avonmore's* situation — their cash-flow cycle, integrating
> Calderwood, the combined group's cash and FX position. Not the bank's product catalogue.
> Each option shows what it would cost, what it would return, *and* whether credit is likely
> to clear it — so Daisy knows what's realistic before she ever proposes it. She can explore
> freely in draft mode without touching the CRM, and if she needs specialist input, she loops
> in the right product colleague with one click. The bank's economics are shown — but they
> follow the client's need, they don't lead it."

**The point.** RMs shape deals around client needs; REO does the analysis and the credit
readiness. Client strategy leads, bank economics follow.

---

### Scene 6 · Post-meeting Momentum
*Captured once — available to everyone who needs it.*

**In one sentence.** The conversation is captured once and flows automatically to everyone
who needs it — no re-keying, and Daisy's next action is already waiting.

**What's on screen.** An auto-generated deal summary synced to CRM minutes after the
meeting: key moments, deal components with values, a task list with owners and due dates,
a notification that Marcus has been looped in, a drafted follow-up email in Daisy's voice,
and a credit narrative REO has already drafted for her to review.

**The talk-over.**
> "The meeting's over. In the old world this is where the admin tax kicks in — writing up
> notes, updating the CRM, chasing colleagues. Here, it's already done. REO captured the
> conversation once and pushed it everywhere it needs to go. The CRM updated itself. Marcus
> — the other RM — has been automatically notified that Calderwood is now part of a group
> mandate, so nothing falls between the two teams. There's a follow-up email already drafted
> *in Daisy's tone of voice*, ready for her to check and send. And REO has even drafted the
> credit narrative. Daisy's job now isn't to write any of this — it's to review it and move."

**The point.** Enter once, flows everywhere. The CRM stops being an admin burden and
becomes a by-product of the conversation.

---

### Scene 7 · Agent Bench & Orchestration
*You orchestrate — agents progress the deal.*

**In one sentence.** Daisy stays in the relationships; REO runs a bench of agents and
colleagues in parallel to drive the deal toward credit.

**What's on screen.** An orchestration board in three tiers — **autonomous** (agents
running on their own), **human-in-the-loop** (agent recommends, Daisy approves), and
**RM-only** (judgement reserved for the human). A credit-progression strip shows stage,
approvals done, and what's still missing. Cross-coverage: Marcus's sign-off appears as a
human-in-the-loop item; a product specialist runs a parallel workstream.

**The talk-over.**
> "This is the engine room. REO has assembled the right team — some are AI agents, some are
> human colleagues — and they're all working the deal in parallel: market research, the
> credit narrative, pricing, product structuring. The board splits the work into three tiers.
> Some things the agents just *do* — autonomously. Some things they recommend, but Daisy
> approves — for example, Marcus's cross-coverage sign-off surfaces here as a decision, not
> as a lost email. And some things stay firmly with the human — the judgement calls. Daisy
> isn't doing the work; she's orchestrating it. That means more time on the conversations
> only she can have, and fewer things falling through the cracks between teams."

**The point.** The RM leads and orchestrates; REO runs the parallel workstreams. One
orchestrator, clear human accountability — not "a swarm of random bots".

---

### Scene 8 · Portfolio Opportunity Radar
*The same play, repeated across your whole book.*

**In one sentence.** What just happened for Avonmore now runs continuously across Daisy's
entire portfolio — surfaced as today's three priorities, not a firehose.

**What's on screen.** A portfolio scorecard (book value vs targets), a smart-filter digest
bar showing "today's three priorities" by default, and a ranked opportunity list — each
card showing the trigger, the reason and the timing, plus what it was detected from. Product
specialists can push ideas into the same list.

**The talk-over.**
> "Everything we've just seen for one client, REO does across Daisy's whole book — always on.
> But here's the important design choice: it does *not* dump every signal on her. By default
> she sees *today's three priorities* — ranked by value and urgency. She can expand to the
> full list when she wants, but the default is a curated digest, because the risk with this
> kind of system is overwhelming the RM, not underwhelming them. Product specialists can also
> push relevant ideas to her through the same surface — so intelligence flows both ways. The
> Avonmore play isn't a one-off. It repeats wherever the conditions are right."

**The point.** Always-on origination that *respects the RM's attention* — a priority list,
not a signal overload. This directly answers the "RM overload" workshop feedback.

---

### Scene 9 · Unified Platform
*One pane of glass — across the whole group.*

**In one sentence.** One live view of the entire Avonmore Group relationship — client, deal
and workflow — with a clear line of sight to what comes next.

**What's on screen.** A single view spanning both RMs and both entities: the deal's position
across the five components (Identify → Qualify → Shape → Handover → Deepen), a full
interaction log across Daisy's and Marcus's threads, the live workflow queue, and forward
markers — next review date, next signal, next recommended action.

**The talk-over.**
> "This is the consolidated view — one place where the whole Avonmore Group relationship lives.
> Both RMs, both companies, one record. You can see exactly where the deal sits in the journey,
> the full history of every interaction across both teams, and the workflow running live in the
> background — REO opening the credit case and routing it, rather than Daisy driving it by hand.
> And notice the framing: this is deliberately *not* the end of the story. The last thing this
> screen shows isn't 'deal done' — it's what comes next: the next review, the next signal, the
> next action. The relationship is always live, not just when a deal is open."

**The point.** One platform, shared across the team, where the relationship is continuous —
and the closing note points *forward*, not backward.

---

### Scene 10 · Relationship Review & Next Cycle
*The deal is live — and the next cycle has already begun.*

**In one sentence.** The mandate is won, and REO has already detected the next opportunity —
so the cycle restarts from a position of strength.

**What's on screen.** A quarterly relationship-review card for the Avonmore Group: the
mandate live, covenant and engagement status, next actions grounded in the full deal history,
and — critically — a fresh signal: a Midlands precision-engineering supplier exploring a sale,
a potential bolt-on to the group. The card mirrors the Scene 1 "why this client, why now" format.

**The talk-over.**
> "And here's the loop closing. The deal is done — but the relationship doesn't go quiet.
> REO keeps watching, and it's already surfaced the *next* opportunity: a precision-engineering
> supplier in the Midlands is exploring a sale — a natural bolt-on to the group Daisy just built.
> Look at the format of this card — it's the same 'why this client, why now' we opened with in
> Scene 1. That's deliberate. The cycle is restarting, but from a much stronger position: all
> the context, all the relationship history, all the knowledge from this deal is retained and
> ready. There's no cold start. The knowledge never walked out the door."

**The point.** The relationship is a *cycle*, not a transaction. It never stops, and the
institutional knowledge compounds instead of leaking away. This is the answer to "close with
the ongoing relationship, not the deal outcome".

## Three messages to land no matter what

If the detail gets lost, these are the three things the whole demo exists to prove:

1. **Human relationship first, technology as the enabler.** Daisy owns the relationship and
   the judgement. REO surfaces, drafts and coordinates — it never replaces her.
2. **One orchestrator, clear accountability.** It's REO end-to-end, with human sign-off where
   it matters — not a scattering of disconnected bots or a black box.
3. **A continuous cycle, not a one-off deal.** The story closes by opening the next chapter —
   the knowledge compounds and belongs to the bank.
